Flagship Project

Nova Control: Concrete Plant Desktop Application

Windows Application · 7 sections · 2,635 tests · v2.0.4

A desktop tool that sends recipe and job details to the concrete plant’s PLC. The operator still decides when to start, stop, or empty the mixer.

The problem

A concrete plant needs to keep recipes, customer orders, trucks, materials, and production records in sync. The difficult part is that the software also communicates with the plant’s PLC, so it must send the right information without being able to operate the machinery itself.

What I built

A Windows application with seven main sections for plant setup, recipes and materials, clients, customer requests, dispatching, live plant information, and reports. It can work with newer Delta controllers and older Omron systems. It also creates concrete passport PDFs, keeps material and activity records, makes backups, and includes a simulated controller for testing without real machinery.

Nova sends the recipe, requested amount, and number of mixer cycles to the plant touchscreen. The operator checks the information and starts production from there. Nova can read what happens and save the results, but it cannot start, stop, empty, reset, or directly control the machinery.

How I built it

I built Nova through many small AI-assisted tasks and review sessions. A concrete-plant expert answered 40 questions about how the real workflow should behave. The project now has 2,635 automated tests covering screens, calculations, saved data, unusual inputs, controller connections, restarts, and simulated production.

Control Boundary

Nova sends recipe and order details → The operator checks them and starts from the plant touchscreen → Nova reads what happened and saves the records. The desktop application cannot operate the machinery.

  • 2,635 automated tests
  • 40 expert answers
  • 7 main sections
  • Delta + older Omron systems
  • Plant setup for different layouts
  • PDF passports + activity records

The Hard Part Making sure the safety rule stayed unchanged as the project grew: Nova may send production details, but the operator remains in control. Every new feature also had to follow the rules confirmed by the plant expert.

v2.0.4: software work complete, real-machine testing still required.

2,635 automated tests · simulated controller checks pass

All seven sections and the connection process work with the simulated controller. Before use at a customer’s plant, Nova still needs to be tested with the real Delta controller and checked with Bulgarian-speaking plant operators.

Fake Discount Bulgaria

Chrome/Edge Extension Live on Chrome Web Store ↗

The extension saves prices from products you visit on 20 Bulgarian stores, then helps you judge whether a discount looks genuine.

The problem

Online stores show discounts in different ways, and the crossed-out price does not always tell the full story. The extension saves the prices a user actually sees over time, so the current offer can be compared with a real personal history.

What I built

A Chrome and Edge extension that saves one EUR price per day when a user visits a product page on one of 20 supported stores. It shows a price-history chart and one of five simple messages: Fake discount, Real deal, Volatile price, Stable price, or Tracking. Users can search their history, set a target price, save product barcodes when available, back up their data, and use the extension in Bulgarian or English.

Each store has its own extraction rules because the websites are built differently. The extension handles pages that change without a full reload, EUR and BGN prices, unavailable products, manufacturer prices, legal 30-day reference prices, and unrelated suggested products. Price records are also sent to an online database when possible, but the extension currently makes its decisions from the history saved in the user’s browser.

How I built it

I used Claude Code and saved copies of real product pages to test the extraction rules repeatedly. The main challenge was teaching the extension how to find the correct product price on 20 very different websites without accidentally reading an old price, an unavailable item, or a suggested product.

  • 20 supported stores
  • 5 clear result messages
  • Daily history in the browser
  • Optional online backup of records
  • Barcode capture when available
  • Bulgarian + English

The Hard Part Finding the correct price on 20 websites that change independently, while still giving the user a short and understandable explanation.

Малки Подаръчета

Ordering Website · 81 pages Live at malkipodaracheta.bg ↗

A simple ordering website for handmade gifts that collects the important details without adding online payment or unnecessary complexity.

The problem

My wife makes handmade gifts for children’s events from a small home workshop. Orders used to be discussed across Facebook and Viber messages, where quantities, dates, names, product choices, and delivery details could easily be missed.

What I built

A live website with 81 pages. Customers choose from six event types and ten themes, creating 60 possible event-and-theme combinations. They then complete three clear steps: choose products, enter personalization details, and review the order.

The cart is saved in the browser, stays in sync between open tabs, can be shared with another person through a link, and checks saved prices against the current product list. The site includes six customizable gift types, 16 variations, 19 ready-made products, 32 previous-project galleries, delivery choices, urgent-order warnings, and volume discounts at 100, 200, and 300 eligible items. The finished order is sent to the workshop by email, then confirmed and paid for directly with the customer.

How I built it

I described the workshop’s prices, discount rules, required order details, and common mistakes to Claude Code and OpenAI Codex. The AI tools built and revised the website, while I tested the complete ordering process on desktop and mobile. The site is hosted without a custom server or payment system because the workshop does not need either.

  • 81 website pages
  • 60 event-and-theme combinations
  • 6 gift types + 16 variations
  • 19 ready-made products
  • 32 project galleries
  • 77 pages listed for search engines

The Hard Part Keeping shared and saved orders accurate without a database. The website must reject old prices, missing products, broken links, and incomplete personalization instead of silently sending a wrong order.

SIMA-MA Industrial Website

Bilingual Industrial Website Staged at Netlify ↗

A Bulgarian and English website that clearly shows what the manufacturer makes, its completed work, and how potential customers can contact the company.

The problem

SIMA-MA makes concrete batching plants, valves, silo equipment, custom steel parts, protective linings, recycling equipment, pontoons, and automation systems. The company needed one clear website for Bulgarian and international customers, project photos, inquiries, and required EU documents.

What I built

A lightweight website with six page types in both Bulgarian and English, for 12 content pages in total. It includes an industrial background animation, a 93-image gallery split into seven categories, a full-screen image viewer that works with a keyboard or phone swipe, and an EU project section with 16 required documents in five groups.

The contact form checks required details, blocks obvious spam, asks for privacy consent, and shows clear errors. Google Maps loads only after the visitor agrees. The hosting setup includes security and caching rules, while the search setup tells Google which pages are Bulgarian or English and how the company and its services should appear.

How I built it

I used Claude Code and OpenAI Codex to build, review, and test the site. I chose simple website files instead of a large framework so future text, images, and documents are easier to update.

  • 12 Bulgarian/English pages
  • 93 optimized project images
  • 7 gallery categories
  • 16 EU documents
  • Keyboard + swipe image viewer
  • Security and caching settings

The Hard Part Turning incomplete company information and many technical photos into a clear website, then keeping the Bulgarian and English versions consistent.
